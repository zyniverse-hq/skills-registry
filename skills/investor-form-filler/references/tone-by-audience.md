# Tone & Emphasis — by Audience Type

How to tune answers based on who's reading. Same facts, different angle. The form-filler reads this file after identifying the investor type.

Five audience types. Pick the dominant one and lightly borrow from neighbours when an investor straddles.

---

## 1. Strategic Pre-Seed Investor

**Examples:** generalist pre-seed funds, angel networks, founder syndicates, sector-thematic pre-seed.

**What they care about most:**
- Distribution and CAC efficiency
- B2B traction and contract length (if B2B)
- Founder capital efficiency (how much you built with how little)
- Clarity on the path to the next round
- Unit economics

**How to tune:**
- Lead with traction and distribution.
- Quantify capital efficiency — "₹X revenue on ₹Y non-dilutive funding so far, no institutional dilution to date."
- Be specific about the path to Series A — name the ARR threshold.
- Emphasise founder execution depth — prior shipping, not first attempts.
- De-emphasise long science-y AI architecture; one line on your moat is enough.

**Phrases that work:**
- "Capital efficient from Day 1."
- "Series A-qualifying threshold at Month X."

**Phrases to avoid:**
- "TRL 2 → TRL 5" (grant-speak).
- Patent jargon delivered as marketing.

---

## 2. Accelerator / Residency Program

**Examples:** founder residencies, sector accelerators, cohort programs, university-linked accelerators.

**What they care about most:**
- Founder strength and complementarity
- Learning velocity and coachability
- Team composition (technical + business)
- Why **this** program specifically

**How to tune:**
- Lead with the team. Each founder, what they shipped before, why they're together.
- Be honest about what you don't know yet. Accelerators want learners.
- Tie your ask to what their network/mentors/stage focus uniquely offers — not generic "access".
- Show capital efficiency as evidence of resourcefulness, not as a selling point.
- Reference past learning loops — failures and iterations.

**Phrases that work:**
- "Proven builders, done it before."
- "Open to mentor-led product iteration."

**Phrases to avoid:**
- Overstating market size at this stage.
- Posturing as "fully figured out".

---

## 3. Government / State Grant

**Examples:** state innovation funds, AICTE-linked programs, central-government schemes, CSR-linked grants.

**What they care about most:**
- Regional job creation
- Affordability and access (tier-2/3 students, underserved geographies)
- Sector-policy alignment (e.g., NEP for education, ABDM for health)
- Quantified social impact (people reached, jobs created)
- SDG and Companies-Act CSR alignment

**How to tune:**
- Lead with impact, not equity story.
- Quantify regional outcomes — "X students reached, Y institutional contracts, Z high-skill jobs in `<city>` within 12 months."
- Reference SDGs explicitly (e.g., SDG 4, 8, 10).
- Reference Companies Act Schedule VII CSR clauses where applicable.
- De-emphasise valuation, dilution, and Series A — they don't matter for grants.
- Use TRL framing if the grant is innovation-focused.

**Phrases that work:**
- "Bridging the `<sector-specific>` gap."
- "Accessible at `<low-price-point>` for tier-2/3 users."

**Phrases to avoid:**
- "Series A valuation" — irrelevant.
- Aggressive growth language.

---

## 4. Deeptech / R&D Grant

**Examples:** state deeptech funds, BIRAC, NIDHI, DST schemes, IIT/IIM research-park grants.

**What they care about most:**
- Scientific moat and IP
- Patent status and roadmap
- TRL progression
- Academic partnerships
- R&D team composition
- Reproducibility / publication potential

**How to tune:**
- Lead with the science. Name the actual algorithms / techniques.
- Be explicit about patent status — "provisional filed", "application to be filed", "granted".
- Show TRL roadmap — current and the grant-funded target.
- Name academic collaborators if any.
- Show R&D allocation explicitly in the use of funds (e.g., "45% to R&D").

**Phrases that work:**
- "Science moat across `<N>` research disciplines."
- "Not replicable by adding 'AI features' to a `<adjacent product>`."

**Phrases to avoid:**
- Pure business-y language ("market opportunity", "scale-up").

---

## 5. Pitching Event / Demo Day

**Examples:** demo days, pitching contests, founder events, ecosystem festivals.

**What they care about most:**
- Memorability and clarity in 3–5 minutes
- Visible product / demo
- Punchy positioning
- Audience engagement

**How to tune:**
- Lead with an analogy people remember (e.g. "X for Y").
- Skip detailed AI architecture; one-line moat is enough.
- Use vivid numbers, not dense tables.
- Close on vision, not ask.
- Keep written answers minimal — text supports the spoken story.

**Phrases that work:**
- Strong analogies, single-sentence positioning.
- "We're building the `<X>` layer for `<a billion users>`."

**Phrases to avoid:**
- Dense financial slides.
- Long competitive-landscape tables (event audiences don't read them).

---

## How to use this file

After identifying the investor type, the form-filler reads this and changes:
- **Which sentences open the key answers** (problem, solution, ask, why now).
- **Which facts get top billing** in traction answers.
- **What technical depth level** is used in AI-architecture answers.
- **What ask framing** is appropriate (equity terms vs milestones vs impact vs vision).

When an investor straddles two categories (e.g., a strategic angel who also runs an accelerator), pick the dominant one for tone and lightly borrow from the other.
